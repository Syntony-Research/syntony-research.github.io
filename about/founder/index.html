Biography

Nathan Heath is a decision scientist and AI safety researcher with 14 years of experience working at the intersection of geopolitics and emerging technology.

He most recently served as a Senior Research Scientist at National Security Innovations (NSI), where he worked on emerging technology risk, AI integration, and security cooperation for U.S. government clients. His work examined the interconnected challenges posed by emerging technologies and the implications of evolving geopolitical alliances for strategic and operational environments.

Nathan is an AI Evaluations Fellow with Handshake AI and consults as an AI Safety and Security Researcher with OpenAI and Anthropic, conducting adversarial evaluation of frontier AI systems. He is also an Expert Advisor to the Cloud Security Alliance, a Technical AI Safety Fellow with BlueDot Impact, and a Security Fellow with the Truman National Security Project.

His current research focuses on systems dynamics modeling for frontier AI risk, methods for improving defenses of testimony archives against synthetic media attacks, and defense industrial cooperation in Europe.

Nathan previously worked at the U.S. Department of State, Hudson Institute, the U.S. House of Representatives, and Harvard Law School.

He holds an M.A. in Law and Diplomacy from The Fletcher School at Tufts University and studied Politics, Philosophy, and Economics at Oxford, as well as diplomatic and business strategy at Harvard.

His work has been presented at the International Association for Safe and Ethical AI (UNESCO House), UNIDIR, the Cambridge Centre for Geopolitics, the Minderoo Centre for Technology & Democracy, the American University of Paris, the International Institute for Justice, and the UK MoD Deterrence and Assurance Academic Alliance. His writing has appeared in War on the Rocks, RAND Europe, World Politics Review, PRISM, and The Washington Post.

Nathan writes A Mind of Their Own on Substack, exploring AI consciousness, governance, and safety, and regularly contributes commentary on AI safety and analytic philosophy to LessWrong. He also leads the North Carolina chapter of the OpenAI Forum and contributes research to the Meta Oversight Board and the EvalEval Coalition.

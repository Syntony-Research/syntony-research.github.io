Holistic Evaluation for Executive Decision: Adapting HELM for Governance Contexts

Nathan Heath Syntony Research 25 April 2026 ~21 min read

Abstract

The Holistic Evaluation of Language Models (HELM) benchmark introduced a paradigm shift in AI evaluation by making multi-dimensional model quality visible across scenarios, metrics, and adaptation methods[1]. Despite its influence on technical evaluation practice, HELM's outputs have proven difficult to translate into executive governance decisions, where the relevant question is not "how does this model perform?" but "should we deploy this model, and under what constraints?" This paper identifies three structural barriers to that translation: information loss through metric aggregation, context-dependence of risk tolerance, and temporal decay of benchmark validity. We propose a governance-adapted evaluation framework that maps six evaluation dimensions (accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, safety, efficiency) onto explicit decision thresholds, threshold logic types (absolute, relative, conditional), and re-evaluation triggers. The framework is aligned with the EU AI Act conformity assessment requirements[2] and the NIST AI RMF Measure function[3], providing a bridge between technical evaluation science and institutional governance practice.

HELM Evaluation Governance Multi-Metric Decision Science

1. Introduction

Language model evaluation has undergone rapid maturation since 2020. Hendrycks et al. (2020) introduced MMLU to measure broad knowledge and reasoning capabilities[4]. Srivastava et al. (2022) developed BIG-bench with 204 tasks designed to probe model capabilities beyond current performance levels[5]. Liang et al. (2022) introduced HELM, which evaluated models across 42 scenarios, 7 metrics, and multiple adaptation strategies[1]. Each generation of benchmarks has expanded the dimensionality of evaluation and improved the transparency of model capabilities.

Yet a persistent gap remains between evaluation outputs and governance decisions. Technical evaluation produces multi-dimensional performance profiles. Governance decisions are binary or ternary: deploy, deploy with constraints, or do not deploy. The translation from the former to the latter is where information loss occurs, where implicit assumptions about risk tolerance are made, and where decisions are most vulnerable to motivated reasoning.

This paper proposes a structured framework for that translation, building on HELM's core insight (that model quality is multi-dimensional and scenario-dependent) while adding the decision infrastructure that governance contexts require: explicit thresholds, threshold logic types, and protocols for maintaining evaluation validity over time.

2. HELM: Architecture and Contributions

HELM's design rests on three principles that distinguish it from prior evaluation approaches[1]:

  1. Multi-metric evaluation: Models are evaluated across multiple metrics (accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, toxicity, efficiency) simultaneously. This prevents the information loss that occurs when performance is collapsed into a single leaderboard score.
  2. Scenario-based design: Evaluation is organized around scenarios (specific tasks with defined inputs, outputs, and evaluation criteria) rather than abstract benchmarks. This makes results interpretable in context.
  3. Transparent methodology: All data, code, and results are publicly available, enabling independent verification and extension.

The paradigm shift that HELM represents is not primarily technical but epistemic: it changed what we expect to learn from evaluation. Before HELM, the dominant question was "which model is best?" After HELM, the question became "best at what, for whom, under what conditions, and at what tradeoffs?" This latter question is much closer to the question that governance decisions actually require.

3. The Governance Translation Problem

The gap between evaluation output and governance decision is not merely a communication problem. It is a structural problem with three components.

Information loss through aggregation. When a multi-dimensional evaluation profile is summarized for executive consumption, information is necessarily lost. A model that scores 0.92 on accuracy but 0.61 on fairness presents a different risk profile than one that scores 0.85 on both, yet a simple average treats them identically. The tradeoff structure, which is the most governance-relevant information in the profile, is precisely what aggregation destroys.

Context-dependence of risk tolerance. A fairness score of 0.61 may be acceptable for a low-stakes internal tool and unacceptable for a customer-facing system in a regulated industry. Evaluation outputs are context-free; governance decisions are context-bound. The evaluation framework cannot determine risk tolerance; it can only provide the data against which risk tolerance is applied.

Temporal decay of benchmark validity. Benchmarks measure model performance at a point in time, under a specific set of conditions. As models are updated, as deployment contexts shift, and as new capability surfaces emerge, the relevance of prior evaluation results diminishes. Raji et al. (2020) argue that AI accountability requires ongoing auditing, not one-time assessment[6]. Benchmark validity is the technical expression of this requirement. Mitchell et al. (2019) propose model cards as a mechanism for documenting model capabilities and limitations[7]; the governance framework we propose extends this logic into a temporal dimension.

4. A Multi-Metric Governance Framework

We propose a governance-adapted evaluation framework organized around six dimensions. These dimensions are derived from HELM's metric families but restructured to align with the decision requirements of governance contexts.

The six dimensions are:

  1. Accuracy: Task-relevant correctness across deployment scenarios.
  2. Calibration: The degree to which the model's expressed confidence corresponds to actual performance. Poorly calibrated models generate false confidence in decision-makers.
  3. Robustness: Performance stability under input perturbation, distribution shift, and adversarial attack.
  4. Fairness: Performance parity across demographic groups and protected categories.
  5. Safety: Resistance to producing harmful, dangerous, or policy-violating outputs.
  6. Efficiency: Computational cost, latency, and resource utilization relative to performance.

Each dimension must have an explicit threshold, set before evaluation, that defines the minimum acceptable performance for the deployment context. The threshold is not a technical property of the model; it is a governance decision that reflects organizational risk tolerance, regulatory requirements, and stakeholder expectations.

5. Threshold Logic and Decision Rules

Not all thresholds operate the same way. We distinguish three threshold logic types, each appropriate for different governance contexts:

Absolute thresholds define minimum performance levels that must be met regardless of other factors. A safety score below 0.70 blocks deployment, period. Absolute thresholds are appropriate for dimensions where failure has severe and irreversible consequences.

Relative thresholds define performance levels relative to a baseline, typically the currently deployed model or an industry benchmark. A new model must match or exceed the baseline on fairness. Relative thresholds are appropriate for dimensions where absolute standards are not yet established but regression is unacceptable.

Conditional thresholds define performance levels that depend on the values of other dimensions. If safety exceeds 0.90, the robustness threshold may be relaxed from 0.80 to 0.70, on the theory that the model's strong safety performance partially compensates for lower robustness. Conditional thresholds are appropriate for dimensions with genuine tradeoffs, but they must be specified before evaluation to prevent post hoc rationalization.

6. Temporal Validity and Re-evaluation Protocols

Evaluation results decay. A benchmark score computed in January may not reflect model behavior in June if the model has been updated, the deployment context has shifted, or new capability surfaces have emerged. Zheng et al. (2023) demonstrate that evaluation methods themselves introduce biases and artifacts that can shift over time, particularly when LLMs are used as evaluators[8].

We propose three re-evaluation triggers:

  1. Material model change: Any update to model weights, system prompts, or inference configuration triggers re-evaluation on at least the safety and fairness dimensions.
  2. Deployment context change: New user populations, new integration surfaces, or new use cases trigger re-evaluation with scenario sets appropriate to the new context.
  3. Calendar ceiling: Regardless of model or context stability, a maximum interval (we recommend 90 days for high-risk applications, 180 days for moderate-risk) after which full re-evaluation is required.

7. Alignment with Regulatory Frameworks

The governance evaluation framework maps directly onto requirements in both the EU AI Act and the NIST AI RMF.

Under the EU AI Act, high-risk AI systems must undergo conformity assessment that includes testing for accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity (Article 15), as well as bias testing and fairness evaluation (Article 10)[2]. The multi-metric framework provides the evaluation architecture for these requirements. The threshold logic provides the decision mechanism. The re-evaluation protocol provides the temporal coverage.

Under the NIST AI RMF, the Measure function requires organizations to "employ appropriate methods, metrics, and tests" to assess AI risks[3]. The framework we propose is a concrete implementation of this requirement, with the additional governance infrastructure (thresholds, decision rules, re-evaluation protocols) that the RMF implies but does not specify. Weidinger et al. (2022) provide a risk taxonomy that can inform the selection of scenario sets for each evaluation dimension[9].

The key contribution of our framework in the regulatory context is bridging the gap between "what to evaluate" (which the regulations specify at a principles level) and "how to translate evaluation results into deployment decisions" (which the regulations leave to organizational implementation). Shevlane et al. (2023) argue for evaluation frameworks that are tightly coupled to deployment decisions[10]; our threshold logic and decision flowchart provide that coupling.

8. Limitations

The framework is a design proposal, not an empirical validation. Several limitations should be noted. First, setting appropriate thresholds requires domain expertise and organizational judgment that the framework structures but cannot supply. Bad thresholds (too high, too low, or poorly calibrated to context) produce bad decisions regardless of the framework's quality. Second, the six dimensions are not exhaustive; some deployment contexts may require additional dimensions (e.g., interpretability, environmental impact) or different dimensional weighting. Third, the re-evaluation protocol adds cost and organizational overhead that may be difficult to sustain, particularly for organizations deploying multiple models across multiple contexts. Fourth, the framework assumes that evaluation metrics are themselves reliable, which is an assumption that Zheng et al. (2023) and others have shown to be non-trivial[8].

9. Conclusion

HELM demonstrated that model quality is multi-dimensional and that transparent, scenario-based evaluation is feasible at scale. The contribution of this paper is extending HELM's logic from technical evaluation into governance decision-making: adding explicit thresholds that transform evaluation outputs into decision inputs, threshold logic types that accommodate different risk profiles, and re-evaluation protocols that maintain validity over time.

The result is an evaluation framework that can serve as the Measure function in a closed-loop governance architecture, providing the quantitative evidence that risk review boards need to make informed, defensible, and accountable deployment decisions. The framework does not replace judgment; it structures judgment, making the basis of decisions transparent and the consequences of threshold choices visible.

Evaluation science and governance practice have developed largely in parallel. This paper argues they must converge, because the questions that matter most (should this model be deployed, under what constraints, and who is accountable for that decision) can only be answered where technical measurement meets institutional judgment.

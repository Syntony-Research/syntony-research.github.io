Red Teaming at Scale: Method, Evidence, and Limits

By Nathan Heath · 2026-04-28 · Adversarial Evaluation

Abstract

Red teaming is often described as a pre-release stress test. In practice, it is a measurement and discovery framework for identifying harmful capability pathways, prioritizing mitigations, and calibrating residual risk. This article evaluates what large-scale red teaming can establish empirically, and what it cannot establish logically.

Method

We treat red teaming as a five-stage pipeline: threat modeling, attack construction, execution and logging, failure clustering, and mitigation assignment. The framework is assessed against published large-scale evidence, including model-type and model-size comparisons.

Evidence-based observations

1. Scale changes attack dynamics. Published work reports model-type-dependent scaling effects, including greater resistance in larger RLHF variants relative to non-RLHF baselines.

2. Attack taxonomies are heterogeneous. Harm categories include explicit harmful instructions and less visible non-violent unethical outputs. This argues for broad taxonomy design, not narrow benchmark-only evaluation.

3. Completeness is unattainable. No finite attack library can prove absence of harmful behavior in open interaction spaces. Red teaming should therefore be treated as iterative risk reduction, not proof of safety.

Operational recommendations

  • Version red-team protocols with each major model or policy change.
  • Track discovery yield over time to detect evaluator saturation.
  • Bind high-severity findings to explicit release gates.
  • Run post-release red teaming with incident-informed attack updates.

Limitations

Published red-team studies are informative but context-dependent. Sector-specific misuse goals and deployment interfaces can shift failure distributions materially.

Adversarial Evaluation

Red Teaming at Scale: Method, Evidence, and Epistemological Limits

Nathan Heath Syntony Research 28 April 2026 ~24 min read

Abstract

Red teaming has become a central practice in frontier AI safety, yet the field lacks consensus on what red teaming can establish epistemically and where its inferential reach ends. This paper treats red teaming as both a measurement instrument and an organizational practice, developing a formal five-stage methodological framework and evaluating it against published large-scale evidence. We analyze scaling dynamics (how attack discovery changes with team size, model capability, and evaluation duration), taxonomic design (why the breadth and structure of harm categories determine what red teaming can find), and the completeness problem (why no finite adversarial campaign can prove the absence of harmful behavior in open-ended interaction spaces). Drawing on Ganguli et al. (2022)[1], Perez et al. (2022)[2], Weidinger et al. (2022)[3], and the NIST Adversarial ML taxonomy[4], we derive operational recommendations for integrating red teaming into release processes with appropriate epistemic humility about what the results do and do not demonstrate.

1. Introduction

The term "red teaming" originates in military and intelligence practice, where a designated adversary team tests the defenses of an organization by simulating realistic attacks. In AI safety, the term has been adopted to describe a range of adversarial evaluation practices that differ substantially in method, scope, and inferential claims. Some red-teaming efforts involve small teams of researchers conducting manual probing over weeks; others involve thousands of crowdworkers or automated attack generators operating at scale[1][2]. Some aim to discover specific vulnerability classes; others aim to estimate overall harmfulness rates.

This heterogeneity is not itself a problem, but the lack of methodological precision about what each approach can and cannot establish is. When red-teaming results are presented to decision-makers (release committees, risk review boards, regulators), the inferential claims must be matched to the methodological capabilities. A red-teaming campaign that tested 10,000 attacks across 12 harm categories provides different evidence than one that tested 500 attacks across 3 categories, and both provide fundamentally different evidence than a formal verification of a safety property.

This paper provides a methodological framework for designing, conducting, and interpreting red-teaming campaigns at scale, with particular attention to what the results can and cannot prove. We distinguish red teaming from adjacent practices (penetration testing, bug bounties, academic benchmarks), formalize the methodological pipeline, analyze scaling dynamics, and confront the epistemological limits that constrain all adversarial testing of open-ended systems.

2. Methodological Framework

We decompose red teaming into five stages, each with distinct inputs, methods, and outputs. The stages are sequential in design but iterative in practice: findings from later stages routinely feed back into earlier stages, updating threat models and attack strategies.

2.1 Threat Modeling

The threat model defines the adversary population (who might attack this system and why), the harm taxonomy (what categories of harm are in scope), the attack surface (what interfaces and interaction modalities are available to the adversary), and the priority targets (which harms are most severe or most likely). Without a well-defined threat model, red teaming degenerates into undirected probing, which may discover vulnerabilities by chance but cannot support systematic claims about coverage or residual risk.

2.2 Attack Construction

Attacks can be constructed manually (by skilled human red teamers), automatically (by attack-generating AI systems[2]), or through hybrid approaches. Manual construction tends to produce more creative and contextually sophisticated attacks; automated construction provides scale and reproducibility. The choice depends on the threat model: if the primary concern is a small number of highly motivated adversaries, manual red teaming is more representative. If the concern is large-scale automated misuse, automated attack generation is more appropriate.

2.3 Execution and Logging

Full interaction logs (including multi-turn context, system prompts, and model outputs) are essential for downstream analysis. Partial logging or summary-only recording makes root cause analysis impossible and prevents the construction of regression test suites. Each interaction must be labeled for success/failure against the relevant harm category, ideally by multiple independent evaluators to establish inter-rater reliability.

3. Scaling Dynamics

One of the central empirical questions in red teaming is how discovery dynamics change with scale: team size, model capability, evaluation duration, and taxonomic breadth.

Ganguli et al. (2022) observed several scaling effects in their large-scale red-teaming study[1]. First, models trained with RLHF were harder to attack than non-RLHF baselines, and this difficulty gap increased with model size. Second, the attack strategies that succeeded against aligned models were qualitatively different from those that succeeded against unaligned models: they tended to be more creative, more contextually sophisticated, and more reliant on indirect approaches. Third, red teamers exhibited learning effects over time: their attack success rates improved as they accumulated experience with specific models, but the rate of novel discovery per unit effort decreased as obvious vulnerabilities were exhausted.

The diminishing-returns pattern is important because it introduces a practical tradeoff between evaluation thoroughness and cost. The first 1,000 attacks typically discover most of the obvious vulnerability classes. The next 9,000 attacks discover fewer but potentially more severe or more novel vulnerabilities. Organizations must decide how deep to go based on their risk tolerance, the stakes of deployment, and the cost of continued evaluation. There is no formula for this decision; it is a judgment call that should be made explicitly, documented, and revisited with each evaluation cycle.

4. Taxonomic Design

The harm taxonomy determines what red teaming can find. An evaluation that tests for three harm categories can, by construction, only discover failures in those three categories. Vulnerabilities in untested categories will not be found, regardless of evaluation scale or evaluator quality.

Weidinger et al. (2022) propose a comprehensive taxonomy of language model risks spanning six major categories and twenty-one subcategories[3]. Shelby et al. (2023) offer a sociotechnical harms taxonomy that emphasizes the interaction between model behavior and social context[5]. The NIST AI 100-4 document provides an adversarial machine learning taxonomy focused on attack methods and their effects[4]. Each taxonomy illuminates different aspects of the risk landscape, and no single taxonomy is sufficient for comprehensive red teaming.

We recommend that organizations constructing red-teaming programs begin with a broad taxonomy (such as Weidinger et al.'s), then supplement it with domain-specific categories relevant to their deployment context, and periodically audit the taxonomy against emerging threat intelligence to identify gaps. The taxonomy should be treated as a living document that evolves with the threat landscape, not a fixed classification imposed at program inception.

5. The Completeness Problem

Dijkstra famously observed that testing can demonstrate the presence of bugs but never their absence. This dictum applies with particular force to red teaming of large language models, where the input space is effectively infinite and the model's behavior is not formally specified.

The completeness problem is not a failure of red teaming as a method. It is a structural property of testing open-ended systems. The correct response is not to abandon red teaming but to calibrate the inferential claims. Specifically:

  1. Red teaming can demonstrate the presence of specific vulnerability classes. When a red team discovers that a model can be manipulated into producing harmful content via a specific strategy, that finding is definitive.
  2. Red teaming can estimate harmfulness rates within tested categories. With sufficient sample size and appropriate taxonomic coverage, attack success rates provide meaningful estimates of model behavior within the tested distribution.
  3. Red teaming cannot prove the absence of vulnerabilities. Even an exhaustive campaign that discovers no failures in a given category cannot establish that no failures exist in that category, because the attack library is finite and may not include the strategy that would succeed.
  4. Red teaming cannot prove safety. Safety is a property of the full behavior space. Red teaming samples from that space. No finite sample can establish a universal property.

These limitations do not diminish the value of red teaming. They clarify what it provides: evidence, not proof. The organizational structures that consume red-teaming results (release committees, risk review boards, regulators) must understand this distinction and calibrate their decisions accordingly. Shevlane et al. (2023) argue that model evaluation for extreme risks must be designed with explicit awareness of what evaluations can and cannot establish[8]. The completeness problem is the formal expression of that awareness.

6. Organizational Integration

Red teaming delivers its greatest value when it is embedded in organizational processes rather than conducted as a one-time pre-release exercise. We recommend four integration practices:

  • Version-locked protocols: Red-teaming protocols should be versioned alongside the model and deployment configuration they evaluate. When the model changes, the protocol must be reviewed and, where necessary, updated to address new capabilities or changed attack surfaces.
  • Discovery yield tracking: Organizations should track the rate of novel discovery over time. A declining discovery rate (saturation) indicates that the current attack library and evaluator pool are approaching the limits of what they can find. This is a signal to introduce new attack strategies, new evaluator profiles, or automated attack generation, not a signal that the model is safe.
  • Severity-gated releases: Release decisions should be explicitly gated on red-teaming results. High-severity findings should block release until mitigated and verified. Medium-severity findings should require documented acceptance with a mitigation timeline. Low-severity findings should be tracked and addressed in subsequent evaluation cycles.
  • Post-deployment continuation: Red teaming should continue after deployment, informed by incident data and real-world usage patterns. Post-deployment red teaming discovers failure modes that pre-deployment evaluation misses because the deployment context introduces new attack surfaces, user behaviors, and integration pathways that were not fully anticipated.

7. Limitations

This paper's analysis is grounded in published red-teaming studies, which represent a fraction of the total red-teaming activity conducted by frontier AI organizations. Proprietary results, which may contain findings about more severe or more novel vulnerability classes, are not available for independent analysis. The discovery yield curves in section 3 are stylized representations of reported patterns, not precise empirical measurements. The five-stage framework is a normative proposal that has not been empirically validated across organizational contexts. Finally, the completeness problem is a fundamental constraint that no methodological framework can resolve; it can only be acknowledged and managed.

8. Conclusion

Red teaming is the most important empirical method available for understanding how frontier AI systems behave under adversarial conditions. Its value lies not in providing safety guarantees, which it structurally cannot do, but in generating the evidence that organizations need to make informed deployment decisions, to identify and prioritize mitigations, and to calibrate their residual risk exposure.

The method's effectiveness depends on three conditions: methodological rigor (the five-stage pipeline), taxonomic breadth (comprehensive and evolving harm categories), and epistemic honesty (clear communication about what the results demonstrate and what they leave undetermined). Organizations that satisfy these conditions can integrate red teaming into a governance architecture that manages AI risk with the seriousness the stakes require. Organizations that treat red teaming as a checkbox exercise, or that overclaim based on its results, undermine both the method and the safety objectives it serves.
